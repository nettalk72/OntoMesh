The Desire for Completion

Ontology, Logic, and the Limits of Definition

From philosophy to formal systems, from Gödel to artificial intelligence, this series follows a single word we have never stopped using— “completion.”

Not as a statement of truth, but as a decision.
Not as an end, but as a way to continue working despite knowing that the end can never be reached.

This is not a defense of ontology, nor an attack on it.
It is a record of following one persistent doubt to its limit.

Preface

— When the Word “Completion” Began to Sound Strange

The first time I heard the phrase “Ontology Completion,” what unsettled me was not that I failed to understand a technical term, but that the expression was used with such disarming naturalness.

Completion.

Completion of what, to what extent, and by which criteria?

These questions are usually treated as insignificant. Ontologies, we are told, are bounded by domains; completion is merely a practical concern; to invoke philosophical implications is considered excessive.

Yet it was precisely this point that troubled me.

Humanity has already experienced, once before, what happens when the word “completion” is taken seriously— and has suffered a logical defeat because of it.

In the early twentieth century, Russell and Whitehead attempted to complete all of mathematics through logic. Their ambition was not excessive; it was, in fact, the most intellectually honest aspiration of their time.

And Gödel proved that this ambition did not fail because it was naïve or insufficient, but because it succeeded.

Completion did not collapse from the outside; it imploded from within.

After that moment, philosophy split. One path began to doubt systems themselves. The other, rather than abandoning totality altogether, chose to refine individual sentences, individual rules, with extreme precision.

What we now call analytic philosophy is, in fact, the outcome of that choice.

It is not a philosophy that denied completion, but one that succeeded in narrowing its scope.

And that philosophy flowed, almost seamlessly, into computer science.

Which is why this question cannot be avoided:

Is ontology completion truly a state of knowledge?

Or is it a language devised to allow work to continue, even after we already know that completion is impossible?

These texts begin with that question.

They are not an attempt to deny ontologies, nor to attack AI or formal logic.

Rather, they seek—at least once— to follow to the end the word we use too casually: “completion.”

To ask when, why, and in what sense it managed to survive.

This series does not promise conclusions. It is closer to a record of pursuing a single doubt to its limit.

We already know that completion is not possible.

And yet, we continue to speak of it.

If this question no longer feels trivial to you, you will already read these words to the end.

Part I

Why Do We Want to Speak of “Completion”

— The Desire Called Ontology Completion

The word “completion” is a strange one.

It feels especially strange when encountered in technical documents.

Systems expand, requirements change, domains are always partial and provisional— and yet, in the midst of all this, we often say: “This ontology is now complete.”

This sentence does not report a fact. It is closer to a declaration of attitude.

From a technical perspective, ontology completion usually has a fairly modest meaning.

There are no missing concepts that block inference; the rules are closed; and for any given query, the system can respond without stalling.

In short, it means “sufficiently defined.”

The problem begins here.

Between saying “it is sufficient” and saying “it is complete,” there is a leap that logic does not require.

Why do we not simply say “sufficient”? Why insist on saying “complete”?

This question points beyond the technical domain.

The word completion functions as a form of permission: permission to stop touching the system, a declaration that the current state may serve as a reference point, and above all, a spell that temporarily pushes uncertainty aside.

It is not a word used for systems, but for human reassurance.

What is striking is that the word completion appears most frequently in fields that are, by their nature, premised on incompleteness.

Software never stops updating. Data is always insufficient. Any attempt to model the world is inevitably smaller than the world itself.

And yet, we continue to invoke the word “completion.”

This is not so much a misunderstanding born of ignorance as it is a choice repeated with full awareness.

The word completion carries an implicit promise:

What has been defined here will no longer be questioned.

This promise is powerful.

It halts questions, closes discussion, and allows us to move on to the phase of calculation and application.

What ontology completion actually accomplishes is not a final declaration about what exists, but a signal that the phase of work has shifted.

This is why ontology completion is less a metaphysical claim than an operational decision.

“This should be enough.”
“Let’s put it into operation.”
“If we dig any deeper, the schedule collapses.”

The word completion is the refinement of all these statements into a single term.

But it is precisely here that discomfort arises.

The word ontology originally belonged to philosophy— a language for speaking about being.

And the word completion implies that no further questions about that being remain.

The moment the two are combined, a technical decision begins to take on ontological authority.

It no longer sounds like “this is how we have defined it,” but rather, “this is how it exists.”

What this series calls into question is not the act of constructing ontologies.

That act is unavoidable, and often useful.

The problem lies in the name we give to that act.

The word completion does not describe a state of knowledge so much as it reveals a way of managing anxiety.

We do not stop because we know the world completely; we declare that we are stopping because we no longer wish to remain unsettled.

This declaration does not fail— because it was never aimed at truth to begin with.

Completion is not a fact. It is a decision.

And that decision has already once led humanity into a vast experiment in thought.

In the next part, we will look back to a time long before ontology became a technical term, to trace where this ancient obsession with classifying being began.

Beginning with the question of why, when faced with the world, we so persistently feel the desire to put things in order.

Part II

Ontology Was Originally Philosophy

— Humanity’s Long Obsession with Classifying Being

Ontology was not, to begin with, a technical term.

Long before the word technology itself existed, humans were already asking ontological questions.

What exists?
And by what criteria can those things be distinguished?

These were not questions of idle curiosity.

In order to survive, to judge, and to render the world intelligible, humans had to divide something from something else.

In ancient Greece, philosophy was, before it was a theory of the world, a technique of ordering.

Aristotle’s categories were not ornaments designed to decorate explanations of being. They were tools for enduring confusion.

Is this a substance or an attribute?
A particular or a universal?
Accidental or essential?

These distinctions were less a claim that “the world truly is structured this way” than a confession that without such divisions, thinking itself was impossible.

What matters here is that in these early forms of ontology, the concept of completion is largely absent.

Being was classified, but those classifications were always provisional.

Philosophy after Aristotle refined or revised his categories, but it never declared, “This is finished.”

Ontology was not a closed system, but something closer to a map continuously amended and extended.

With the arrival of the medieval period, ontology became intertwined with theology.

Here, being was no longer understood merely as that which exists, but as that which is created, ordered, and placed within a hierarchy.

It is at this moment that the idea of a complete inventory of being first begins to appear.

God knows everything. Everything already has its assigned place.

Yet paradoxically, this completion did not belong to humans.

Human ontology remained incomplete, and under the shadow of divine completion, it was required to remain humble.

With the advent of modernity, the situation changes.

As the place of God is emptied, something else must step in to occupy it.

Reason.

Philosophy after Descartes constructs an ontology not around the world itself, but around the knowing subject.

What exists becomes less important than what can be stated with certainty.

Being is no longer merely classified; it becomes something that must be proven.

At this point, ontology quietly changes its goal.

It moves from the task of listing the world to the task of grounding the world completely.

This shift creates the conditions under which the word completion can later appear as something natural.

Being is no longer treated as an open question, but as a list that could, in principle, be fully reached—given sufficient method.

Within philosophy itself, however, this belief does not last long.

Any criterion for classifying being always presupposes another criterion, which in turn demands explanation.

Ontology increasingly reveals that it cannot justify itself.

Classification was necessary, but final classification was impossible.

And yet, this ancient obsession does not disappear.

Instead, it changes form.

Attempts to classify the whole world become too large and too dangerous, and so they retreat into smaller, safer territories.

Domains.
Formal systems.
Closed languages.

Ontology withdraws from the world and enters the system.

This movement is not a defeat, but a strategic retreat.

Even while accepting that the whole cannot be spoken, there still must be places where order is possible.

And it is precisely at this point that ontology leaves philosophy and moves toward logic and mathematics.

In the next part, we will examine the moment when this movement appeared in its most optimistic form: the attempt of Russell and Whitehead.

Why they believed, “This time, it really is possible,” and why that belief remains the most refined failure of all.

Part III

Russell and Whitehead: The Last Optimism Toward Completion

— Principia Mathematica as a Great Failure

At the beginning of the twentieth century, mathematics was unstable.

Contradictions were not accidental mishaps; they arose repeatedly from within the system itself.

Anxiety about foundations continued to grow.

Russell and Whitehead did not look away from this anxiety. They confronted it directly.

For them, the problem lay not in calculation, but in foundations.

Their goal was clear:

To reduce all of mathematics to logic.

Numbers, sets, functions, proofs— all of these, they believed, could be rigorously derived from a small number of logical axioms and rules.

If this were possible, mathematics would no longer tremble.

Principia Mathematica was the product of this ambition.

This attempt was not reckless.

By the standards of the time, it was the most conservative and the most thorough option available.

Intuition was excluded. Self-evidence was treated with suspicion. Every step was written out explicitly.

The reason it took hundreds of pages to prove that 1 + 1 = 2 was not exaggeration, but a refusal to take anything for granted.

What Russell and Whitehead believed in was not completion, but grounding.

Once the foundations were perfectly secured, everything built upon them would follow mechanically.

This belief touches a kind of ontological optimism:

the conviction that being, once translated into a sufficiently well-defined language, would no longer waver.

What matters is that this optimism did not arise from naivety.

Russell already knew, through his own paradox, how easily a system could collapse.

And yet, he did not flee.

Instead, he pushed the paradox into the system itself, constructing the more refined apparatus of type theory.

A stance that acknowledges failure without surrendering.

Principia Mathematica achieved a great deal.

The refinement of logical notation.
The establishment of standards for formal proof.
The entrenchment of the attitude that everything must be stated explicitly.

Much of what we now take for granted in formal logic and programming languages traces back to this work.

For that reason, this book is not a failure.

And yet, this attempt failed.

More precisely, it failed because it succeeded.

Russell and Whitehead pressed their own rules to the limit.

As a result, what the system could do and what it could not do became all too clear.

Principia Mathematica was not a complete system.

But it became the stage on which the impossibility of completion was demonstrated with the greatest persuasiveness.

When a system becomes sufficiently precise, its limits become precise as well.

Those limits are no longer voiced as external criticism, but are prepared to be spoken in the system’s own language.

Onto this prepared stage, Gödel appears.

He does not arrive bearing a new philosophy.

He adds no new axioms.

He simply uses the language Russell and Whitehead had constructed to say what that language itself could not say.

In the next part, we will examine this quiet but fatal turn:

the fact that completion did not collapse from the outside, but imploded from within.

A proof that required no new axioms to undo axioms themselves— Gödel’s moment.

Part IV

Gödel: Completion Collapses from Within

— A Proof That Undermined Axioms Without Adding Any

Gödel’s name is often summarized as that of “the man who showed that mathematics has limits.”

But this description is too quick.

What Gödel did was not to discover limits from the outside, but to follow—step by step— how a system already in operation betrays itself from within.

He did not throw stones from the outside.

He entered the system, spoke only in its language, and constructed a sentence that the language itself could not bear.

Gödel’s first incompleteness theorem is technically complex, but its core is simple.

If a formal system is sufficiently powerful, consistent, and capable of speaking about itself, then within that system there must exist a sentence that is true but unprovable.

What matters is not merely that such a sentence cannot be proven, but that the system cannot exclude it.

Gödel constructed, within the system itself, the sentence: “This sentence cannot be proven.”

If the sentence is proven, the system falls into contradiction.

If it is not proven, the system must concede its incompleteness.

Either way, completion is impossible.

This is not an external attack.

It is a blank space the system leaves for itself.

There is a common misunderstanding here.

Gödel did not leave us with the simple lesson that “we should just add more axioms.”

Adding axioms may expand a system.

But in that expanded system, the same problem reappears.

Completion is merely postponed, never reached.

This is why Gödel’s proof is so devastating.

Incompleteness is not a temporary flaw, but a structural condition.

The more powerful a system becomes, the more clearly it delineates what it cannot say.

The closer one moves toward completion, the more precisely the impossibility of completion is demonstrated.

At this point, the attempt of Russell and Whitehead begins to look different.

Their failure was not the result of excessive ambition, but the unavoidable outcome of pushing that ambition all the way through.

The moment a system takes completion as its goal, it must explain itself completely.

And at precisely that moment, something unexplainable inevitably remains.

After Gödel, the word completion can no longer be used innocently.

When someone speaks of a completed system, they mean one of two things.

Either they have defined what completion means with extreme restriction,

or they already know that completion is impossible, and are using the word for some other purpose.

Philosophy after Gödel finds itself at this fork.

One path says: “Then the very idea of a system must be the problem.”

The other says: “Then let us say, with absolute precision, only what a system can say.”

These two choices divide the course of twentieth-century philosophy.

In the next part, we will examine the second path—

the road that abandons totality and chooses the sentence: the attitude of analytic philosophy.

Why, even after Gödel, logic and formalism were not discarded.

Part V

The Choice After Failure: Analytic Philosophy

— Abandoning the Whole, Choosing the Sentence

Even after Gödel, logic and formalism did not disappear.

This fact is often overlooked.

Even after the impossibility of completion was proven, people continued to use logic, to posit axioms, and to refine formal systems.

This was not inertia.

It was a deliberate choice.

Gödel’s theorems were not a declaration that “nothing can be done.”

They merely stated that “the whole cannot be reached in this way.”

And so the question changes:

If the whole is impossible, what, then, can we do?

Analytic philosophy is the tradition that answered this question with the greatest sobriety.

At its core, analytic philosophy is a reduction of ambition.

The whole world ❌
The whole of language ❌
The essence of meaning ❌

Instead:

When is this sentence true?
In which cases does this rule apply?
Under what conditions does this expression function?

Completion was abandoned, but precision was not.

Early Wittgenstein’s Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus is an extreme example of this attitude.

The world is the totality of facts.

What can be said must be said clearly.

And of what cannot be spoken, one must remain silent.

All the tension of the post-Gödel moment is condensed here.

After failing to speak of the whole, the decision is made to clarify—without remainder— only what can be said.

Analytic philosophy then moves toward ever smaller units.

Semantics.
Syntax.
Logic.
Speech acts.
Reference.
Truth conditions.

These are less tools for revealing the essence of the world than devices for removing misunderstanding.

Analytic philosophy does not explain.

It organizes.

This attitude aligns with computer science to a surprising degree.

Computers do not understand the whole.

They merely follow rules.

If a sentence is ambiguous, computation halts.

If a definition is missing, the system collapses.

And so the language of analytic philosophy— formality, explicitness, closed rules— is naturally absorbed into computable languages.

A crucial shift occurs here.

For analytic philosophy, formalization was an attitude.

For computer science, formalization is a condition.

What in philosophy meant “let us speak this way” becomes, in engineering, “unless we speak this way, nothing works.”

The philosophical strategy of abandoning completion ironically makes local completion possible.

From this point on, the word completion returns with a different meaning.

Not the completion of the world, but the completion of a sentence.

Not the totality of being, but the closure of a domain.

In ways analytic philosophy never intended— yet precisely because of its attitude— “completion” becomes speakable again.

But this completion is no longer naïve.

It is not a claim about the whole, nor a declaration of truth.

It says only this:

Within this language, under these rules, for this purpose, no further questions need be asked.

This is not a philosophical statement, but an operational one.

In the next part, we will examine this very moment—

the point at which the attitude of analytic philosophy hardens into the language of engineering.

Is ontology completion the heir of philosophy, or the most misunderstood legacy philosophy has left behind?

Let us dissect it directly.

Part VI

The Nature of Ontology Completion

— The Language of Engineering, Not of Philosophy

The phrase ontology completion has a curiously neutral sound.

It is neither excessive nor aggressive.

And for that very reason, it is easily accepted.

It appears as if it emerged naturally from purely technical necessity, untouched by long-standing philosophical disputes.

But this term is not so pure.

Ontology completion is not a discovery, but a declaration of state.

Not “this ontology is true,” but “this ontology is now sufficiently defined.”

And the criterion of sufficiency here does not come from the world, but from the requirements of the system.

Does inference terminate?
Are there unresolved exceptions?
Does the system always respond to queries?

When these questions can be answered with “yes,” we say that the ontology is complete.

At this point, a crucial assumption is silently adopted:

The Closed World Assumption.

What is not stated is treated as non-existent.

Philosophically, this is an extremely strong claim.

Engineering-wise, without it, the system simply does not function.

Ontology completion is the procedure by which this assumption is formalized.

What matters is that completion here is entirely independent of truth conditions.

A completed ontology can be wrong.

It can be incomplete.

It may even diverge from reality.

And yet, it is still called complete.

Because the goal is not understanding, but computation.

It is at this point that philosophy and engineering decisively diverge.

Philosophy asks:
Does this really exist?
Is this classification justified?

Engineering asks:
Does the system terminate?
Can we proceed to the next step?

Ontology completion is an answer to the latter.

For this reason, ontology completion is less an extension of philosophical ontology than the result of converting the attitude of analytic philosophy into operational rules.

It eliminates ambiguity, closes definitions, and forbids exceptions.

This is not a language for speaking about being, but a language for finishing work.

The problem is that this language works too well.

Declaring completion halts discussion, enables deployment, and draws boundaries of responsibility.

“Any problems beyond this point are no longer problems of the ontology.”

This sentence is technically legitimate, but philosophically heavy.

Ontology completion feels unsettling not because it imitates philosophy, but because it reuses, without explanation, a word philosophy had already abandoned.

After Gödel, the word completion was something to be handled with care.

Engineering, however, redefines the word for a different purpose, and then uses it as if nothing had happened.

This reuse is not deception.

It is a choice made out of necessity.

But every choice comes with a cost.

Ontology completion does not tell us what we have given up.

It foregrounds only one fact: that the system works.

In the next part, we will examine what kinds of fractures appear when this language encounters AI.

Why artificial intelligence, which began with the languages of analysis and formalization, increasingly turns toward ways of handling meaning outside formal systems.

And whether, by drawing every leaf perfectly, the forest truly comes into view.

Part VII

The Arrival of AI: Was Analysis Really the Answer?

— If Every Leaf Is Drawn Perfectly, Does the Forest Appear?

For a long time, analytic philosophy and engineering were looking in the same direction.

Remove ambiguity. Make definitions explicit. Close the rules— and meaning will follow.

Early artificial intelligence inherited this belief almost intact.

The world could be represented in symbols. Symbols could be manipulated by rules. And with a sufficiently refined ontology, intelligence could be implemented.

This approach was reasonable.

And it achieved real results.

Expert systems.
Knowledge-based reasoning.
Rule engines.
Ontology-centered knowledge representation.

Here, intelligence was measured not by understanding, but by application.

Correct classification.
Consistent inference.
Predictable behavior.

Yet the same problems followed this approach everywhere.

As ontologies grew, maintenance costs exploded.

When the world shifted even slightly, rules quickly became obsolete.

Most of all, the meanings people actually used constantly crossed the boundaries of ontologies.

Analysis became more refined, but intelligence did not grow as well as expected.

The turning point arrived in an unexpected form.

Models that relied on statistics— later, deep learning and large language models— did not begin by constructing ontologies.

They did not classify being. They did not define meaning.

Instead, they absorbed patterns.

This approach is almost the opposite of the analytic attitude.

Explicit rules ❌
Closed definitions ❌
Declarations of completion ❌

Instead:

Probability.
Continuity.
The coexistence of ambiguity.

AI does not define each individual leaf.

It looks first at the distribution of the forest as a whole.

At this point, an uncomfortable question emerges.

Was the assumption we held for so long— that “sufficiently precise analysis, accumulated enough, will reveal the whole”— ever really correct?

No matter how precisely one draws each leaf, the climate or ecology of the forest may still remain invisible.

Of course, this new kind of AI does not solve everything.

Its explanations are weak. Its grounds are opaque. The boundary between truth and error is often blurred.

And yet, one thing has become clear.

Meaning does not arise only within closed ontologies.

This realization forces us to look again at ontology completion.

If completed definitions are the precondition of intelligence, then what are these systems that function without them?

Conversely, if such systems can handle meaning, what, then, was the completion we were so obsessed with for?

This is not to say that analytic philosophy was wrong.

It accomplished precisely what it set out to do.

What has changed is that its limits are now unmistakable.

Analysis was one mode of intelligence, not intelligence itself.

The arrival of AI calls for a philosophical return.

Not the question of completed definitions versus perfect understanding, but the question of how to work together under the assumption of incompleteness.

In the next part, we will push this question to the end.

After letting go of the old ideal of completion, what kind of ontology can we still have?

Not completion, but coexistence as an alternative.

Part VIII

Back to the Whole: Coexistence Instead of Completion

— Toward a New Concept of Ontology

To abandon completion does not mean to abandon the whole.

What we gave up was not the whole itself, but the belief that we could possess a completed whole.

This difference is not trivial.

After Gödel, we already know:

No system can fully explain itself.
No ontology can completely close off what lies outside it.

And yet, we continue to build ontologies.

This is not a contradiction. It is a necessity.

The problem is not which ontology we have, but what kind of relationship we form with it.

The moment completion becomes the goal, ontology turns into a device for closing questions.

When coexistence is assumed, ontology becomes a device for coordinating questions.

A coexistential ontology has the following characteristics:

  • It assumes no single final classification.
  • It allows the overlap of multiple perspectives.
  • It treats conflict not as error, but as information.
  • It prioritizes connection over closure.

Such an ontology asks less “What is this?” and more “Where and how is this used?”

Here, the whole is not the goal, but the background.

A forest is not the sum of its leaves. But without leaves, there is no forest.

The whole is not a structure to be completed, but a field in which parts continually affect one another.

AI offers an experimental glimpse of this possibility.

Large-scale models operate meaningfully without presupposing a single ontology.

They form context not through definitional closure, but through the coexistence of patterns.

This is not the end of ontology, but a transformation of its role.

Ontology Completion is still necessary.

Systems must, at some point, be closed. Decisions must, somewhere, be made.

But such completion should not be understood as a declaration of truth, but as the drawing of a boundary of responsibility.

“Up to this point, we take responsibility.”

Beyond that, we must negotiate with other ontologies.

Ontology no longer needs to be singular.

Multiple ontologies can coexist simultaneously, even without full translation between them.

What matters is the meta-relationship that allows movement between them.

Overlapping maps reveal more than a single completed one.

This series was not written to deny the idea of completion.

It was written, rather, to return that idea to its proper place.

Completion is not a statement about the world, but the name of a way of working that we choose.

We still want to organize.
To classify.
To close.

That desire does not disappear.

But now, we no longer need to mistake it for something absolute.

The moment we choose coexistence over completion, ontology becomes once again a tool for thinking.

Not a tool for closing, but a tool for connecting.

Epilogue

Writing “Completion” One Last Time

If we were to write the word “completion” just once more, it would be like this.

We tried to draw a single map while facing the world, and to stamp the word “completion” at its edge.

But the moment the brush touched, the canvas exceeded its own boundary and revealed a wider margin.

That margin is not a lack, but a space where questions not yet asked are breathing.

“Completion” is no longer an ideal that has vanished without remainder.

It remains as a single sentence in which we declare how far we take responsibility, and from where we accept the questions of others.

A promise not to cross one line, and at the same time, an invitation to let it overlap with someone else’s.

So let us leave the final page blank.

When someone writes a new question upon that empty space, this map will expand once again.

That is the only way in which we can be eternal.

The only form of completion left to us.

— End.
